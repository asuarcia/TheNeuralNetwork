export const meta = { title: "The Persona-Driven Interview Simulator" };

You'll turn an AI into a strict, in-character interviewer that runs you through a realistic mock interview and scores your answers. It's a practical study in reliable, bounded roleplay. The interview happens in a real chat assistant; this notebook helps you design the rubric and tally the result.

## 1. Define the interviewer

The magic is a system prompt that locks the model into a role *and* a process. Build the core instruction.

<CodeExercise
  id="is-prompt"
  prompt="Assemble the interviewer's system prompt from its rules."
  starter={`role = "You are a senior interviewer for a data analyst role."

scoring = "After 5 questions, score each answer 1-5 and give brief feedback."

print(system_prompt)`}
  solution={`role = "You are a senior interviewer for a data analyst role."
rules = "Ask one question at a time. Wait for my answer. Never reveal the answer."
scoring = "After 5 questions, score each answer 1-5 and give brief feedback."
system_prompt = f"{role} {rules} {scoring}"
print(system_prompt)`}
/>

<Callout type="tip">
**Do this now:** Paste that prompt into your chat tool and answer its questions one at a time. Test the boundaries: try asking it for the answer, or to skip ahead. A well-built persona will politely refuse and stay in role — that reliability *is* the project.
</Callout>

## 2. Score the session

When the interview ends, collect the per-question scores and compute your overall result.

<CodeExercise
  id="is-score"
  prompt="The interviewer scored your five answers. Print the average, rounded to 2 decimals."
  starter={`scores = [4, 3, 5, 4, 4]
average = sum(scores) / len(scores)
print(round(average, 2))`}
  expected="4.0"
  solution={`scores = [4, 3, 5, 4, 4]
average = sum(scores) / len(scores)
print(round(average, 2))`}
/>

An average of 4.0 across five questions — and because the persona scored consistently against a rubric, you can re-run it and track improvement over time.

## 3. Make it adaptive

A great simulator gets *harder* when you do well. Add to the system prompt: "If my last answer scored 4 or 5, make the next question harder; if it scored 1 or 2, ask a simpler follow-up." Now the difficulty tracks your skill.
