Before transformers, machines generated text with **Markov chains** — learning which words tend to follow which, then sampling. You'll build one here and watch it babble. It's generative AI stripped to its essence. Run the cells in order.

## 1. Learn the transitions

Walk through a corpus and record, for each word, the list of words that follow it. That table *is* the model.

<CodeExercise

  prompt="Build the transition table and print the words that follow 'the'."
  starter={`corpus = "the cat sat the cat ran the dog sat".split()
transitions = {}
for current, nxt in zip(corpus, corpus[1:]):
    transitions.setdefault(current, []).append(nxt)

print(transitions["the"])`}
  expected="['cat', 'cat', 'dog']"
  solution={`corpus = "the cat sat the cat ran the dog sat".split()
transitions = {}
for current, nxt in zip(corpus, corpus[1:]):
    transitions.setdefault(current, []).append(nxt)
print(transitions["the"])`}
/>

After "the" the corpus goes to "cat", "cat", then "dog" — so the model will pick "cat" twice as often as "dog". Frequency *is* probability here.

## 2. Generate text

Start from a word and repeatedly sample the next one from its transition list. Run it (we seed the randomness so your result is reproducible).

<CodeExercise
  id="mk-generate"
  prompt="Generate a short sequence by sampling the transition table."

corpus = "the cat sat the cat ran the dog sat".split()

word = "the"
out = [word]
for _ in range(6):
    if word not in transitions:
        break

random.seed(1)

That's text generation: model the distribution of "what comes next," then sample from it — exactly what an LLM does, just with vastly more context and learned probabilities instead of raw counts.
