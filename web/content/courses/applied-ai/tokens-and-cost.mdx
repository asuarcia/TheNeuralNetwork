Two things in an LLM app are measured in **tokens**: what you *pay*, and what *fits*. Everything in this lesson comes back to those two facts.

## A quick refresher

A token is a word-piece — roughly ¾ of a word on average. "Hello" is one token; "internationalization" might be four. A useful rule of thumb:

> **~1,000 tokens ≈ 750 words ≈ 1.5 pages of text.**

Both your input (the prompt) and the model's output count as tokens, and they're usually priced differently — output tokens often cost 2–4× more than input.

## Estimating cost

Pricing is quoted per 1,000 tokens (or per million). To estimate a call's cost: convert each side to thousands, multiply by its price, and add.

<CodeExercise
  id="cost-calc"
  prompt="Compute the total cost of one call and print it rounded to 4 decimals."
  starter={`input_tokens = 1500
output_tokens = 500
in_price = 0.005    # dollars per 1,000 input tokens
out_price = 0.015   # dollars per 1,000 output tokens
# Print the total cost, rounded to 4 decimals
`}
  expected="0.015"
  solution={`input_tokens = 1500
output_tokens = 500
in_price = 0.005
out_price = 0.015
cost = (input_tokens / 1000) * in_price + (output_tokens / 1000) * out_price
print(round(cost, 4))`}
/>

That's 1.5¢ for one call. Trivial — until you multiply by a million users. Cost modeling *before* you ship is how you avoid a five-figure surprise.

## The context window: a hard ceiling

A model can only "see" a fixed number of tokens at once — its **context window** (e.g., 8K, 128K, or more). That window must hold *everything*: the system prompt, the conversation history, any retrieved documents, **and** room for the reply. Exceed it and the call fails or silently truncates.

## "Lost in the middle"

A bigger window isn't a free lunch. Models attend most reliably to the **beginning** and **end** of their context, and can overlook facts buried in the **middle** of a long prompt — the "lost in the middle" effect.

Practical consequences:

- Put the most important instructions and the most relevant retrieved chunks near the **top or bottom**, not the middle.
- Don't dump your entire knowledge base into the prompt "just in case." More context can mean *worse* answers and a bigger bill.
- Retrieving the *few* right chunks beats stuffing in *all* of them — which is exactly why RAG exists.

<Callout type="tip">
Think of the context window like RAM, not a hard drive. It's fast, finite, and you actively manage what's loaded. The skill of LLM engineering is deciding what earns a spot in that window on each call.
</Callout>
