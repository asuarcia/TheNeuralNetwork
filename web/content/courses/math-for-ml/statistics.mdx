Raw data is a heap of numbers. **Statistics** turns that heap into a few meaningful summaries, and reshapes it so a model can learn from it fairly. Two ideas do most of the work: measuring the center, and measuring the spread.

## Center: the mean

The **mean** (average) is the center of mass of your data — the single number that best represents "typical." You've computed it before; here it's the anchor everything else compares against.

## Spread: variance and standard deviation

The **variance** measures how spread out the data is: the average of the squared distances from the mean. Its square root, the **standard deviation**, is in the same units as the data, so it's easier to interpret — "most values fall within one standard deviation of the mean."

<CodeExercise

  prompt="Compute the variance of the data (the mean of the squared distances from the mean). Round to 2 decimals."
  starter={`data = [2, 4, 6]

# variance = average of (x - mean)**2 over the data. Print it rounded to 2.
`}
  expected="2.67"
  solution={`data = [2, 4, 6]
mean = sum(data) / len(data)
var = sum((x - mean) ** 2 for x in data) / len(data)
print(round(var, 2))`}

## Normalizing: putting features on equal footing

Imagine predicting house prices from *square meters* (values like 50–200) and *number of bedrooms* (values like 1–5). The big numbers can drown out the small ones just because of scale. **Normalizing** fixes this. A common method is the **z-score**: subtract the mean and divide by the standard deviation, so every feature is centered at 0 with a spread of 1.

```text
z = (value − mean) / standard_deviation
```

<CodeExercise
  id="stat-zscore"
  prompt="Compute the z-score of x = 6 given a mean of 4 and a standard deviation of 2."
  starter={`x = 6
mean = 4
std = 2
# z = (x - mean) / std
`}
  expected="1.0"
  solution={`x = 6
mean = 4
std = 2
print((x - mean) / std)`}
/>

A z-score of 1.0 means "one standard deviation above average." Now every feature speaks the same language, and the model can weigh them fairly.

<Callout type="tip">
Normalizing inputs is one of the highest-impact, lowest-effort steps in all of ML. Skip it and training can be slow or unstable; do it and models often learn faster and better.
</Callout>

## Distributions

Finally, data tends to follow **distributions** — shapes that describe how values cluster. The famous one is the **normal (bell-curve) distribution**: most values near the mean, rare values far out. Lots of natural data is roughly normal, and many ML methods quietly assume it.
