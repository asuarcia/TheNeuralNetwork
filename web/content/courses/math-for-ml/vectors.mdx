Machine learning is built on **linear algebra** — but you need far less of it than a textbook implies. At its heart, linear algebra is about **vectors** (lists of numbers) and **matrices** (grids of numbers), and a handful of operations you can learn in an afternoon.

## A vector is a list of numbers

You met vectors in the Python track; here we make them precise. A vector is an ordered list of numbers, and in ML it represents *one thing* described by several measurements — a house as `[size, bedrooms, age]`, or a word as a long list of learned numbers (an embedding).

```python
u = [1, 2, 3]
v = [4, 5, 6]
```

## Adding and scaling

Two operations come up constantly. **Addition** combines vectors slot by slot. **Scaling** multiplies every number by one value (a *scalar*) — stretching or shrinking the vector.

## The dot product: the most important operation

The **dot product** multiplies two vectors slot-by-slot and sums the result, giving back a single number. It quietly powers similarity search, every neuron, and the attention inside transformers.

```text
u · v = u₁v₁ + u₂v₂ + u₃v₃ + ...
```

A large dot product means the vectors "point the same way" — they're similar. A dot product near zero means they're unrelated.

<CodeExercise
  id="vec-dot"
  prompt="Compute the dot product of u and v (1·4 + 2·5 + 3·6)."
  starter={`u = [1, 2, 3]
v = [4, 5, 6]
# Print the dot product
`}
  expected="32"
  solution={`u = [1, 2, 3]
v = [4, 5, 6]
print(sum(a * b for a, b in zip(u, v)))`}
/>

<Callout type="tip">
A single neuron computes <code>dot(weights, inputs) + bias</code>. So the dot product isn't abstract math — it's the literal operation happening billions of times inside every model you use.
</Callout>
