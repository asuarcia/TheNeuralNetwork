Getting a model to work in a notebook is the start, not the finish. Shipping AI that people rely on means owning what happens after launch.

## Four things that separate a demo from a product

- **Evaluation** — measure quality on a real test set *before* shipping, and re-check after every change. "It felt good in the demo" is not a metric.
- **Monitoring** — watch latency, cost, error rates, and quality drift in production. Models degrade as the world changes.
- **Safety & bias** — check that the system behaves fairly across groups, and add guardrails for harmful or out-of-scope requests.
- **Privacy** — be deliberate about what user data you send to a model or store, and for how long.

<Callout type="tip">
The cheapest time to catch a problem is before launch (evaluation); the next cheapest is the moment it starts (monitoring). Everything after that is damage control.
</Callout>

## Your turn — a monitoring check

<CodeExercise
  id="ship-latency"
  prompt="Print how many requests exceeded the latency budget (in ms)."
  starter={`latencies = [120, 800, 200, 950, 300]
budget = 500
# Print how many requests were slower than the budget
`}
  expected="2"
  solution={`latencies = [120, 800, 200, 950, 300]
budget = 500
print(sum(1 for ms in latencies if ms > budget))`}
/>

That counter, tracked over time and alerted on, is a real production monitor. Build the habit of measuring, and your AI earns trust instead of just demos.
