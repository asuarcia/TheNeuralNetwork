Getting a model to work in a notebook is the start, not the finish. Shipping AI that people rely on means owning what happens after launch — when real users send inputs you never imagined, costs scale, and the world keeps changing.

## What separates a demo from a product

- **Evaluation** — measure quality on a real test set *before* shipping, and re-check after every change (you built this last lesson). "It felt good in the demo" is not a metric.
- **Monitoring** — watch latency, cost, error rates, and quality drift in production. Models degrade as the world changes.
- **Guardrails** — filter harmful or out-of-scope requests on the way in, and unsafe content on the way out.
- **Safety & bias** — check the system behaves fairly across groups, and red-team it for misuse before attackers do.
- **Privacy & governance** — be deliberate about what user data you send to a model or store, for how long, and who's accountable.

<Callout type="tip">
The cheapest time to catch a problem is before launch (evaluation); the next cheapest is the moment it starts (monitoring). Everything after that is damage control — and damage control with AI can mean headlines.
</Callout>

## Monitoring: watch it in production

You can't fix what you can't see. Production AI needs live dashboards on latency, cost per request, error rate, and output quality. Start with the basics — like counting requests that blow the latency budget.

## Detecting drift automatically

Don't wait for users to complain. Compare current quality against your launch baseline and alert when it slips past a threshold — a simple, automatable check.

<CodeExercise
  id="ship-drift"
  prompt="Alert if accuracy has dropped more than 0.05 from the baseline. Print 'alert' or 'ok'."
  starter={`baseline_acc = 0.90
current_acc = 0.78
# Print "alert" if the drop exceeds 0.05, else "ok"
`}
  expected="alert"
  solution={`baseline_acc = 0.90
current_acc = 0.78
print("alert" if baseline_acc - current_acc > 0.05 else "ok")`}
/>

A 12-point drop trips the alert — you find out from your monitoring, not from an angry customer.

## Guardrails and red-teaming

Users (and attackers) will send things you didn't plan for. Defense in depth:

- **Input filtering** — block prompt injection, abuse, and out-of-scope requests.
- **Output filtering** — catch toxic, unsafe, or policy-violating generations before they reach the user.
- **Red-teaming** — deliberately attack your own system to find failures first. Assume someone will try to jailbreak it, because they will.
- **Human-in-the-loop** — require human approval for high-stakes actions (the agent guardrails from Applied AI apply double in production).

## The bigger picture

Responsible AI isn't a checkbox at the end — it's woven through everything you've learned. The risks from Foundations (hallucination, bias, privacy, injection), the honest evaluation from this track, the guardrails from agents — they all converge here. Regulation (the EU AI Act and others) increasingly *requires* this diligence, but the real reason to do it is simpler: systems that are measured, monitored, and guarded earn trust. Systems that aren't, eventually break it.
