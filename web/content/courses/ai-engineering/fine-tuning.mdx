Training a large model from scratch costs millions of dollars and oceans of data. Almost nobody does it. Instead, you take a model that already learned language and **adapt** it to your task — that's **transfer learning**, and **fine-tuning** is how you do it.

## First: do you even need to fine-tune?

This is the most important engineering decision in the track. There's a ladder, cheapest first:

1. **Prompting** — change the instructions. Free, instant. Try this first, always.
2. **RAG** — inject knowledge the model lacks (your docs, fresh facts). Fixes *"it doesn't know X."*
3. **Fine-tuning** — change the model's *behavior, style, or format* by training on examples. Fixes *"it won't reliably act the way I need."*

Fine-tuning is **not** for teaching facts (use RAG) — it's for teaching a consistent *skill or style*: always output valid JSON, adopt a brand voice, classify in your taxonomy, speak a niche domain's dialect.

## How transfer learning works

A pretrained model already encodes enormous general knowledge in its weights. Fine-tuning **freezes** most of that and gently adjusts the model on your (much smaller) dataset, so it keeps what it learned while specializing. You're not teaching English — you're nudging an English-speaker toward your task.

## The dataset is the product

Fine-tuning is only as good as its examples. You prepare **instruction pairs** — input and the ideal output — usually hundreds to thousands of them. Quality and consistency matter far more than raw quantity.

## The dangers

Fine-tuning a giant model on a small dataset is a minefield the ML track prepared you for:

- **Overfitting** — too few examples and the model memorizes them instead of generalizing (high variance — sound familiar?).
- **Catastrophic forgetting** — train too aggressively and the model loses general abilities it had before.
- **Cost & lock-in** — full fine-tuning is expensive to run and produces a whole new copy of the model to host.
