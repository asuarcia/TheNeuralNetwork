**Natural Language Processing (NLP)** is teaching machines to work with human language. But models only do math, so step one — for everything from spam filters to ChatGPT — is turning text into numbers.

## Tokenization: splitting text into pieces

First we break text into **tokens** — usually words (modern systems use word-pieces, as you saw in Foundations). The set of all unique tokens is the **vocabulary**.

<CodeExercise
  id="nlp-vocab"
  prompt="Build the vocabulary size: count the unique words in the sentence."
  starter={`text = "the cat sat on the mat"
# Print the number of unique words (hint: split, then use set)
`}
  expected="5"
  solution={`text = "the cat sat on the mat"
print(len(set(text.split())))`}
/>

Six words, but only five *unique* ones ("the" repeats) — so the vocabulary has 5 entries.

## Bag-of-words: counting tokens

The simplest way to turn a document into numbers is **bag-of-words**: just count how often each vocabulary word appears. The document becomes a vector of counts. It throws away word *order* (hence "bag"), but it's a surprisingly strong baseline for classification.

<CodeExercise
  id="nlp-count"
  prompt="Count how many times 'the' appears in the text — one entry of its bag-of-words vector."
  starter={`text = "the cat the dog the bird"
# Print the count of the word "the"
`}
  expected="3"
  solution={`text = "the cat the dog the bird"
print(text.split().count("the"))`}
/>

## One-hot vectors

To represent a *single* word as a vector, we use **one-hot encoding**: a vector as long as the vocabulary, all zeros except a single 1 at that word's position. It's how words enter a network before being turned into richer **embeddings** (a later lesson).

<Callout type="tip">
Bag-of-words and one-hot are crude — every word is equally distant from every other, so the model has no idea "cat" and "kitten" are related. Fixing that is the whole motivation for embeddings. But for many classification tasks, simple counts already work remarkably well.
</Callout>
