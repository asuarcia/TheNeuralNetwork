Bag-of-words counts have a flaw: the word "the" appears constantly and tells you almost nothing, while a rare word like "refund" might be highly revealing. **TF-IDF** rebalances counts so distinctive words carry more weight.

## Two halves: TF and IDF

- **Term Frequency (TF)** — how often a word appears in *this* document (relative to its length). Common in this doc → higher TF.
- **Inverse Document Frequency (IDF)** — how *rare* the word is across *all* documents. A word in every document → low IDF (boring). A word in few documents → high IDF (distinctive).

Multiply them: **TF-IDF = TF × IDF**. The score is high only when a word is frequent *here* but rare *overall* — exactly the words that characterize a document.

## From features to a classifier

Once each document is a TF-IDF vector, classifying text is just classifying vectors — and you already know how. Feed those vectors to a model from the ML track (logistic regression is a classic choice) with labels like spam/not-spam or positive/negative, and it learns which words push toward which class.

This pipeline — **text → TF-IDF vector → logistic regression** — was the workhorse of practical NLP for years, and it's still a strong, fast, interpretable baseline today.

<Callout type="tip">
Always build the simple baseline first. A TF-IDF + logistic-regression classifier trains in seconds and often gets you 80–90% of the way. Reach for heavy transformers only when that baseline isn't good enough.
</Callout>
