The original email spam filters ran on **Naive Bayes**, and it's still a great first classifier: it's fast, needs little data, and is easy to understand. It's also the probability lesson from the Math track, applied for real.

## The idea

For a new email, ask: is it more probable under "spam" or under "ham" (not spam)? We estimate, for each class, how likely its words are, then pick the class with the higher overall probability.

The **"naive"** part is a simplifying assumption: we treat every word as **independent** of the others. That's not really true ("New York" isn't two unrelated words), but it makes the math easy — and it works shockingly well anyway.

## Independence means multiply

From the probability lesson: the chance of independent events all happening is the **product** of their probabilities. So an email's score under "spam" is the probabilities of its words *given spam*, all multiplied together.

<CodeExercise
  id="nb-product"
  prompt="An email has 3 words. Under 'spam', their probabilities are 0.5, 0.4, and 0.5. Multiply them for the spam score."
  starter={`word_probs = [0.5, 0.4, 0.5]
score = 1.0
# Multiply all the probabilities together. Print the result.
`}
  expected="0.1"
  solution={`word_probs = [0.5, 0.4, 0.5]
score = 1.0
for p in word_probs:
    score = score * p
print(score)`}
/>

## Pick the bigger score

Compute the same product under "ham", then choose whichever class scored higher. That comparison *is* the classification.

<CodeExercise
  id="nb-decide"
  prompt="The email scored 0.1 under spam and 0.05 under ham. Print which class wins."
  starter={`spam_score = 0.1
ham_score = 0.05
# Print "spam" if spam_score is higher, else "ham"
`}
  expected="spam"
  solution={`spam_score = 0.1
ham_score = 0.05
print("spam" if spam_score > ham_score else "ham")`}
/>

<Callout type="tip">
Real implementations add two fixes: <strong>smoothing</strong> (so a never-before-seen word doesn't zero out the whole product) and working with <strong>log-probabilities</strong> (adding logs instead of multiplying tiny numbers, to avoid underflow). The core idea is exactly what you just coded.
</Callout>
