You understand what AI is and how to prompt it. Now let's turn that into time saved on the things you actually do every week. None of this requires technical skills — only the prompting habits you just learned.

## Summarize and digest

AI is excellent at compressing a wall of text into what matters. Paste a long email thread, an article, a report, or meeting notes and ask for exactly the form you need.

> "Summarize this in 3 bullet points, then list any action items with who's responsible."

This works for catching up on a thread you were CC'd on, getting the gist of a long PDF, or turning rambling notes into clean minutes.

## Draft, then make it yours

Staring at a blank page is the hard part — AI is great at the rough first draft you then edit. Emails, posts, cover letters, descriptions, replies.

> "Draft a friendly email declining this meeting and proposing two alternative times next week. Keep it under 80 words."

<Callout type="tip">
**Try this:** Think of an email you've been putting off. Give the AI the situation, the tone you want, and a length limit. Edit the draft it returns. Notice how much faster "edit a draft" is than "write from nothing."
</Callout>

## Brainstorm and unstick yourself

Use AI as a tireless brainstorming partner — then pick the good ideas yourself.

> "Give me 10 names for a neighborhood bakery, ranging from playful to elegant. One line each."

Ask for *more* options than you need, in a range, and then refine: "I like #3 and #7 — give me five more in that style."

## Plan and organize

Turn a fuzzy goal into a concrete plan, schedule, or checklist.

> "Make a one-week study plan for a beginner learning to swim, 30 minutes a day, as a simple day-by-day list."

It's just as good at trip itineraries, event checklists, weekly menus, and project task lists.

## Tidy up information — no spreadsheet skills needed

This surprises people: AI can clean up and reshape messy information using plain English, no formulas required.

- "Take this messy list of names and sort them alphabetically by last name."
- "Turn these notes into a table with columns: Task, Owner, Due date."
- "I want to add up a column in a spreadsheet — what formula do I type?" (it'll explain the exact formula in plain steps).

## One habit to keep: check before you rely

The faster AI makes you, the easier it is to forward something without reading it. For anything that matters — numbers, names, facts, anything you'll send to others — **read it over and verify.** AI gives you a brilliant first draft; you're still the editor. That responsibility is exactly what the final lesson is about.
