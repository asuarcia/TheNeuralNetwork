The message you type — the **prompt** — is the single biggest lever you control. Same AI, better prompt, dramatically better answer. And the good news: prompting well isn't a secret art. It's mostly about **reducing ambiguity** — every useful detail you add removes a way the AI could guess wrong.

## Three habits that fix most bad answers

1. **Be specific about what you want.** "Write about dogs" wanders. "Write three one-sentence tips for house-training a puppy" lands.
2. **Give context.** Say who it's for and any limits — audience, length, tone, format.
3. **Show the shape you want.** Need a list? Ask for a list. Need a table? Ask for a table. Need it short? Say how short.

<Callout type="note">
A vague prompt isn't "easier" for the AI — it's harder. With nothing to go on, it falls back on the most generic, average-sounding answer. Specifics are a gift to both of you.
</Callout>

## A reusable recipe: Role · Task · Format

Almost any request fits a simple pattern. Tell the AI **who** to be, **what** you want, and **how** the answer should look:

> **Role:** You are a friendly fitness coach.
> **Task:** Suggest a 20-minute home workout for a total beginner.
> **Format:** A simple numbered list, no equipment, one line per exercise.

That single structure works for emails, plans, explanations, recipes — almost anything.

<Callout type="tip">
**Try this:** Open any AI assistant and paste a Role · Task · Format prompt for something real in your life — a workout, a meal plan, a tricky email. Then change just the **Format** line ("make it a table," "make it 3 bullets") and watch the answer reshape itself.
</Callout>

## Put your constraints in writing

If you have rules in your head — a word limit, a tone, something to avoid — *write them down*. The AI can't honor a rule it never saw. Just add them to the prompt:

> "...Keep it under 50 words, friendly but professional, and don't use the word 'synergy'."

## Show an example of what "good" looks like

The fastest way to get the format you want is to *show* it. Compare:

> **Vague:** "Give me the details about the event."
>
> **Clear:** "List the event details like this — **Date:** … **Time:** … **Location:** … **Cost:** …"

The second version makes a clean, usable answer the obvious thing to produce.

## Prompting is iteration, not magic

Don't agonize over a perfect first prompt. Write a decent one, look at what's wrong, and add the one detail that would have fixed it. Two or three quick passes beats one "perfect" attempt every time.
