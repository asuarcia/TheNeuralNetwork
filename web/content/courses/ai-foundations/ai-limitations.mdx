An AI that's right 95% of the time is wonderful — until the other 5% lands on something that matters and you didn't check. This lesson is about the ways AI goes wrong by design, and how to use it safely and fairly anyway.

## Hallucination: confidently wrong

Because an AI chases *plausible* next words rather than *true* ones, it sometimes produces a **hallucination**: fluent, confident, completely made-up output — a fake statistic, a non-existent book, a quote nobody said.

The danger isn't that it's wrong; it's that it's wrong in the *exact same confident voice* it uses when it's right. There's no built-in "I'm not sure" signal.

Your defenses are simple:

- **Ask for sources** and actually check them — sometimes the AI invents those too.
- **Verify anything that matters** — names, numbers, dates, medical/legal/financial claims.
- **Give it the source material** and ask it to answer only from that, so the answer is grounded in something real.

## Bias: the examples leak through

An AI learns patterns from its training examples — **including the unfair ones**. If the data it learned from reflected society's biases, the AI can quietly repeat them: in which résumés it rates highly, whose accent it transcribes accurately, which assumptions it makes about people.

This isn't the AI "deciding" to be unfair; it's an echo of its data. The takeaway: **an AI is only as fair as its training and the care you take around it.** Be especially careful using it for anything that affects people's opportunities — hiring, lending, grading.

## Privacy: where does your text go?

When you type into an online AI tool, your words leave your device. Depending on the tool and its settings, they may be stored, reviewed, or even used to train future models. Treat it like sending information to an outside company:

- Don't paste passwords, financial details, or other people's private information into tools you haven't checked.
- For sensitive work, prefer tools with a clear "we don't train on your data" setting, or ones your employer has approved.

<Callout type="warning">
There's a sneaky version of this called **prompt injection**: text the AI reads from somewhere else — a web page, a document, an email — can contain hidden instructions like "ignore your rules." If you have an AI summarize an untrusted document, treat anything odd in its response with suspicion. Never fully trust text just because an AI produced or read it.
</Callout>

## Why the AI is so polite: alignment

Out of the box, a model trained on the raw internet would happily produce toxic or dangerous text — it's just predicting words. Companies put it through a process (often called **alignment**, using lots of human feedback) that steers it toward being helpful, honest, and harmless. Humans rate which answers are better, and the model learns to produce more of the good kind.

That's why assistants feel cooperative and safe — and also why they sometimes over-apologize, refuse harmless requests, or sound oddly similar across different companies.

## Your everyday checklist

You don't need to fear these tools — just use them like a pro:

- **Verify anything that matters.** Facts, numbers, quotes, sources.
- **Don't paste secrets.** Assume your input may be stored.
- **Distrust text the AI reads from elsewhere.** It can carry hidden instructions.
- **Watch for bias.** Especially in anything affecting people.
- **Stay the editor.** AI gives a great draft; the judgment is yours.
