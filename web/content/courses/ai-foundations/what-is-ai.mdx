People throw the word **AI** at everything from chatbots to dishwashers. So let's be precise — in plain language, no math, no code.

Artificial intelligence is software that learns patterns from lots of examples and uses those patterns to make predictions — instead of following step-by-step rules a human wrote by hand. That one idea covers spam filters, recommendation feeds, photo tagging, image generators, and the chatbots you've been talking to.

<Callout type="tip">
The shift that made modern AI possible wasn't smarter rules — it was letting the computer **find the patterns itself** from huge amounts of examples.
</Callout>

## A model is a prediction machine

Strip away the mystique and an AI "model" does one thing: given some input, it predicts a likely output. A chatbot predicts the next words. A photo app predicts "this is a cat." A streaming service predicts what you'll watch next.

It doesn't "understand" the way you do. It has seen so many examples that its guesses are often uncannily good — and sometimes confidently wrong. Holding both of those facts in mind at once is the single most important skill for using AI well.

## Two phases: learning, then using

People constantly mix these up, so it's worth being clear:

- **Training** happens once, ahead of time, and is hugely expensive. The model is shown millions of examples until its guesses match them well. This is the "learning" phase.
- **Using it** (the techies call this *inference*) happens every time you type a message. The learning is already done and frozen — the model just runs your input through and answers.

When you chat with an AI, it is *not* learning from your conversation. It already learned everything it knows, earlier, during training.

<Callout type="note">
This is why a chatbot can have a "knowledge cutoff" and not know recent events. Its knowledge was frozen when training ended — anything newer, it simply never saw. (There are ways to give it fresh information; that's a later track.)
</Callout>

## Not all "AI" is the same

It helps to sort the zoo into a few buckets:

- **Narrow AI** — does one thing well: filter spam, transcribe speech, recommend a song. Everything in use today is narrow.
- **Generative AI** — *creates* new content: text, images, audio, video. Chatbots and image generators live here.
- **General AI (AGI)** — a hypothetical system that matches humans at *any* task. It does not exist today, despite what headlines suggest.

## Try it yourself

You don't need to install anything or write code to start learning AI — you just need to *use* it and pay attention.

<Callout type="tip">
**Try this:** Open a free AI assistant (ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini) and ask it something you already know the answer to — a fact about your town, your job, or a hobby. Notice two things: how natural the answer sounds, and whether every detail is actually correct. That gap between *fluent* and *true* is the thing this whole course teaches you to manage.
</Callout>
