You've felt both halves. Now connect them into the single loop that powers every neural network.

1. A **neuron** (and a network of them) makes a **prediction** from its current weights.
2. A **loss** measures how wrong that prediction was.
3. **Gradient descent** nudges every weight a little downhill to shrink the loss.
4. Repeat — millions of times.

## Half one: the prediction

The network's guess comes from neurons like this. The weights here are *fixed* — this is the forward pass, the "make a guess" step.

The **weights and bias**. The inputs are the data (fixed for a given example); learning means searching for the weights and bias that make the predictions good. That search is gradient descent.

## Half two: the fix

Once we know how wrong the guess was, gradient descent rolls each weight downhill on the loss surface — exactly this motion, happening for every weight at once.

## The whole loop

Guess → measure error → step downhill → guess again. Run that loop enough times and the weights settle into values that make the network's predictions good. There's no magic step hiding anywhere — it's these two things you just played with, repeated at scale.
