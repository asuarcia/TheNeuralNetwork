Training a model means finding the settings that make its error as small as possible. The algorithm that does it — **gradient descent** — is just "feel the slope, take a step downhill, repeat." Let's watch a ball find the bottom of a valley.

<PredictThenReveal prompt="The valley's lowest point is at x = 3. If you crank the learning rate way up (past 1.0), do you think the ball will reach the bottom faster, or something else?">
Something else — it **overshoots**. Too big a step jumps past the bottom to a point that's even higher, then overshoots again, spiraling outward until it flies off the curve. Bigger isn't faster; it's broken. Try it below.
</PredictThenReveal>

## Roll downhill

Press **Run**. Then drag the learning rate and re-run from **Reset**:

- **≈ 0.1** — smooth, steady glide into the minimum.
- **≈ 0.9** — overshoots back and forth but still settles (bouncy).
- **&gt; 1.0** — diverges and explodes off the curve.

<GradientDescentLab />

The yellow arrow always points downhill (opposite the slope). The whole of training is taking that step, over and over, across millions of weights at once.
