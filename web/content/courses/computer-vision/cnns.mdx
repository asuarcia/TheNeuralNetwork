A **Convolutional Neural Network (CNN)** is what you get when you make convolution *learnable* and stack it deep. It's the architecture behind face unlock, medical imaging, self-driving perception, and the image half of multimodal models.

## Layers of learned filters

Each convolutional layer holds many filters, and the network **learns their values** through gradient descent (exactly like the weights in any neural net). Early layers learn simple patterns; later layers combine those into complex ones:

- **Layer 1** learns edges and color blobs.
- **Layer 2** combines edges into corners, curves, and textures.
- **Layer 3+** combines those into parts — an eye, a wheel, a letter.
- **Final layers** combine parts into whole objects — a face, a car, a digit.

This **hierarchy of features** — simple to complex — is why deep CNNs work so well. Nobody programmed "eye detector"; the network discovered it.

## Why not just use a regular (dense) network?

You *could* flatten an image into one long vector and feed it to a normal network — but it falls apart fast:

- **Too many weights.** A 1000×1000 color image is 3 million inputs; a single dense layer would need billions of weights.
- **No sense of place.** A dense layer treats the top-left pixel as unrelated to its neighbor. Convolution builds in **locality**.
- **No reuse.** A cat in the corner and a cat in the center would need separately-learned detectors. Convolution's **weight sharing** detects it anywhere with one filter.

CNNs fix all three: small shared filters, slid everywhere, stacked deep.
