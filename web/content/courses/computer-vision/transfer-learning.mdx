Training a strong vision model from scratch takes millions of labeled images and serious compute. Almost nobody does that. Instead they use **transfer learning** — take a model that already learned to see, and adapt it to your task with a tiny fraction of the data.

## Why it works

Remember the feature hierarchy: early CNN layers learn **general** patterns (edges, textures, shapes) that are useful for *any* image task. Only the final layers are specific to the original goal. So you can keep the general feature-extractor and just replace the part that makes the decision.

The recipe:

1. Take a model pretrained on a huge dataset (like ImageNet).
2. **Freeze** the early layers — keep their learned filters as-is.
3. **Replace the final layer(s)** with new ones for your classes (e.g., "cat vs. dog").
4. **Train only those new layers** on your small dataset.

## Freeze vs. fine-tune

There are two dials:

- **Feature extraction** — freeze everything, train only the new head. Fastest, best when your data is small or similar to the original.
- **Fine-tuning** — unfreeze some later layers too and train them gently (low learning rate). Better when you have more data or a different domain (e.g., medical scans vs. everyday photos).

<Callout type="tip">
Transfer learning is the single most practical idea in applied computer vision — and the same principle powers fine-tuning large language models (the AI Engineering track). "Don't start from zero; adapt something that already learned" is one of the most valuable habits in all of AI.
</Callout>
