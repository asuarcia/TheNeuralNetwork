In 2017 a paper titled *"Attention Is All You Need"* introduced the **transformer**. Within a few years it had taken over language, vision, audio, and code. Every model you've chatted with is a transformer. This lesson is the map of what's inside one.

## Why it replaced what came before

Earlier sequence models (RNNs, LSTMs) read text one word at a time, left to right. Two problems: they were **slow** (no parallelism — each step waited for the last), and they **forgot** — information from early words faded by the end of a long sentence.

The transformer's breakthrough: process *all* tokens **at once, in parallel**, and let every token directly look at every other token through a mechanism called **attention**. No more bottleneck, no more forgetting across distance.

## The pipeline, block by block

Text flows through a transformer like this:

1. **Tokenize & embed** — text becomes tokens, each mapped to a vector (the embeddings you learned in Applied AI).
2. **Positional encoding** — since all tokens are processed at once, the model needs to know their *order*. Position information is added to each embedding.
3. **Attention blocks** — each token gathers relevant information from other tokens (the next lesson, in full).
4. **Feed-forward layers** — a standard MLP (which you just built!) processes each position.
5. **Residual connections + normalization** — keep gradients flowing and training stable through deep stacks.
6. **Output layer** — produces a probability distribution over the next token.

Steps 3–5 form one "block," and a transformer stacks **dozens** of them.

## Order matters: positional encoding

Because attention sees all tokens simultaneously, "dog bites man" and "man bites dog" would look identical without position information. So each token's embedding gets its position folded in. Here's the idea in miniature — pairing each token with its position.

## Residual connections keep it trainable

Stacking dozens of blocks risks the signal (and gradient) getting lost. A **residual connection** fixes this by adding a layer's input back to its output: `output = x + sublayer(x)`. The original signal always has a clear path through, which is what makes very deep transformers trainable.

## Encoder, decoder, or both

Transformers come in three flavors:

- **Encoder-only** (e.g., BERT) — reads a whole input to *understand* it. Great for classification, search, embeddings.
- **Decoder-only** (e.g., GPT) — generates text left to right, predicting the next token. This is what powers chat models.
- **Encoder-decoder** (e.g., T5) — reads an input, then generates an output. Great for translation and summarization.

The decoder-only chat models are **autoregressive**: they predict the next token, append it, and feed the result back in to predict the next — the exact loop from "How LLMs Work," now you know what's generating each prediction.
