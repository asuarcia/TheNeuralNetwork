We've talked about "finding the best `w` and `b`" as if it just happens. It doesn't. There's one algorithm doing that work behind linear regression, logistic regression, and every neural network on Earth: **gradient descent**. Learn it once, and you understand how *all* of them train.

## The loss is a landscape

Picture the loss (MSE, log loss, whatever) as a function of the weights. As you change a weight, the loss goes up or down — tracing out a **surface** with hills and valleys. Training means finding the lowest valley: the weights where error is smallest.

You're standing somewhere on that surface, in a thick fog. You can't see the valley. But you *can* feel the slope under your feet.

## The gradient is the slope

For any weight, the **gradient** is the slope of the loss with respect to that weight: "if I nudge this weight up a little, does the loss rise or fall, and how steeply?"

To *reduce* loss, step in the **opposite** direction of the gradient — downhill. That single rule is gradient descent:

```text
w_new = w - learning_rate * gradient
```

The **learning rate** controls step size. Let's take one step minimizing the simplest loss, `f(w) = w²`, whose gradient is `2w`.

## Watch it roll downhill

Press **Run** and watch the ball descend the loss curve. Then drag the learning rate: around 0.1 it glides in smoothly, near 0.9 it bounces, and above 1.0 it overshoots and explodes off the curve. This one dial decides whether training works at all.

## Iterate to convergence

Run the update over and over and the weight slides down the curve toward the minimum. Watch it converge over three steps from `w = 10`:

<CodeExercise
  id="gd-loop"
  prompt="Run 3 gradient-descent steps on f(w)=w² (gradient 2*w) from w=10.0 with lr=0.1. Print the final w rounded to 2."
  starter={`w = 10.0
lr = 0.1
# Loop 3 times: grad = 2*w; w = w - lr*grad. Then print w rounded to 2.
`}
  expected="5.12"
  solution={`w = 10.0
lr = 0.1
for _ in range(3):
    grad = 2 * w
    w = w - lr * grad
print(round(w, 2))`}
/>

10 → 8 → 6.4 → 5.12, marching toward zero. Give it enough steps and it gets arbitrarily close.

## The learning rate is everything

That one knob makes or breaks training:

- **Too small** — tiny steps; training crawls and may never arrive.
- **Too large** — you overshoot the valley, bounce around, or *diverge* to infinity.
- **Just right** — steady, efficient descent.

<Callout type="note">
Real models have millions of weights, and the gradient is computed for all of them at once. In neural networks, the algorithm that does this efficiently is <strong>backpropagation</strong> — the very first lesson of the Deep Learning track. But the update rule is exactly the one you just wrote.
</Callout>
