You've trained models and measured them. Now the question that separates practitioners from beginners: when a model underperforms, *why*? Almost always, it's one of two opposite failures — and the cure for one makes the other worse.

## Two ways to be wrong

- **Underfitting (high bias)** — the model is too simple to capture the pattern. A straight line through clearly curved data. It does badly on *both* training and test data. It hasn't learned enough.
- **Overfitting (high variance)** — the model is too complex and memorizes the training data, including its noise. It does *great* on training and *badly* on test data. It learned too much of the wrong thing.

The tell is the **gap** between training and test performance.

## Reading the gap

A tiny training error with a large test error is the classic fingerprint of overfitting: the model aced its study notes but flunked the real exam.

That gap of 0.33 is a screaming red flag. Now quantify it directly — the **generalization gap** is just train performance minus test performance.

99% on training, 72% on test — a 27-point gap. The model memorized rather than generalized.

## The tradeoff

Here's the rub: making a model more complex lowers bias but raises variance, and vice versa. You can't drive both to zero. The goal is the **sweet spot** — complex enough to capture the real pattern, simple enough to ignore the noise.

```text
Too simple  ──────────────►  Too complex
high bias        SWEET         high variance
(underfit)        SPOT          (overfit)
```

## The fixes are opposite

Because they're opposite problems, they need opposite cures:

**To fix overfitting (too complex):**
- Get **more training data** — harder to memorize a bigger set.
- **Regularize** — penalize large weights to keep the model humble (L1/L2, dropout in nets).
- **Simplify** — fewer features, a shallower tree, a smaller network.

**To fix underfitting (too simple):**
- Add **complexity** — more features, a deeper model.
- Train **longer** or reduce regularization.

<Callout type="tip">
This single idea reaches all the way up to large language models. "More data and regularization fix overfitting" is why frontier models are trained on enormous, diverse corpora — and it's the backdrop for everything in the AI Engineering track, where you adapt giant models without overfitting your small dataset.
</Callout>
