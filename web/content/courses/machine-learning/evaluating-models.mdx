You can't improve what you don't measure. Before tuning anything, you need numbers that say how wrong your predictions are. The right number depends on whether you're predicting values (regression) or categories (classification).

## Regression: Mean Absolute Error

For predicting numbers, the most intuitive metric is **Mean Absolute Error (MAE)**: for each example, take the gap between prediction and truth, drop the sign, and average.

```text
MAE = average of |prediction - actual|
```

Lower is better. Its units match your label, so "MAE of 3" literally means "off by 3 on average."

<CodeExercise
  id="eval-mae"
  prompt="Print the Mean Absolute Error between preds and actual, rounded to 2 decimals."
  starter={`preds  = [10, 20, 30]
actual = [12, 18, 33]
# Print MAE rounded to 2 decimals
`}
  expected="2.33"
  solution={`preds  = [10, 20, 30]
actual = [12, 18, 33]
errors = [abs(p - a) for p, a in zip(preds, actual)]
print(round(sum(errors) / len(errors), 2))`}
/>

## Classification: why accuracy lies

For categories, the obvious metric is **accuracy** — fraction predicted correctly. But accuracy is dangerously misleading when classes are **imbalanced**.

Imagine a fraud detector where 99% of transactions are legit. A model that screams "legit!" for *everything* scores **99% accuracy** — and catches zero fraud. Useless, but it looks great. To see the truth, we break results into four buckets (the **confusion matrix**):

- **True Positive (TP)** — predicted fraud, was fraud. ✓
- **False Positive (FP)** — predicted fraud, was legit. (false alarm)
- **False Negative (FN)** — predicted legit, was fraud. (missed it)
- **True Negative (TN)** — predicted legit, was legit. ✓

## Precision and recall

Two metrics built from those buckets tell the real story:

- **Precision** = `TP / (TP + FP)` — of everything you *flagged*, how much was right? (Low precision = lots of false alarms.)
- **Recall** = `TP / (TP + FN)` — of everything that *was* positive, how much did you catch? (Low recall = lots of misses.)

## The tradeoff and F1

Precision and recall pull against each other. Flag everything and recall is high but precision craters; flag only the sure things and precision is high but you miss cases. The **F1 score** balances them into one number (the harmonic mean), and the **ROC curve** plots the tradeoff as you slide the decision threshold.

<Callout type="warning">
Always measure on a held-out test set, and pick the metric that matches the <em>cost of being wrong</em>. For cancer screening, missing a case (recall) is catastrophic — tolerate false alarms. For spam, a false alarm (precision) annoys users — be more conservative.
</Callout>
