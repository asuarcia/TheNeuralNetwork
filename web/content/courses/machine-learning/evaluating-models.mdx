You can't improve what you don't measure. Before tuning anything, you need a single number that says how wrong your predictions are. The most intuitive one is **Mean Absolute Error (MAE)**.

## Mean Absolute Error

For each example, take the gap between prediction and truth, drop the sign, and average:

```text
MAE = average of |prediction - actual|, over all examples
```

Lower is better. MAE of 0 means perfect predictions. Its units match your label, so "MAE of 3" literally means "off by 3 on average."

<Callout type="warning">
Always measure error on data the model did <strong>not</strong> train on (a held-out test set). A model graded on its own study notes always looks smarter than it is.
</Callout>

## Your turn

Compute MAE between your model's predictions and the actual values.

<CodeExercise
  id="eval-mae"
  prompt="Print the Mean Absolute Error between preds and actual, rounded to 2 decimals."
  starter={`preds  = [10, 20, 30]
actual = [12, 18, 33]
# Print MAE rounded to 2 decimals
`}
  expected="2.33"
  solution={`preds  = [10, 20, 30]
actual = [12, 18, 33]
errors = [abs(p - a) for p, a in zip(preds, actual)]
print(round(sum(errors) / len(errors), 2))`}
/>

Now you can rank any two models objectively. Optimizing that number is exactly what training a neural network does — next course.
