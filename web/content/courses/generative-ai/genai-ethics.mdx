## Deepfakes and synthetic media

Generative models can fabricate convincing images, audio, and video of real people saying or doing things they never did — **deepfakes**. The upside (film, accessibility, art) is real, but so is the harm: fraud, non-consensual imagery, and political disinformation.

A practical defense is **detection** — flagging content that's likely synthetic when a detector's confidence is high.

## Copyright and training data

Generative models learn from enormous datasets scraped from the internet — often including **copyrighted** art, writing, and code, used without explicit consent. This raises hard, still-unsettled questions:

- Who owns an AI-generated image trained on millions of artists' work?
- Should creators be able to opt out, or be compensated?
- Can a model "memorize" and reproduce training examples too closely?

These are active legal and ethical debates in 2026, with real lawsuits shaping the answers.

## Bias, consent, and provenance

- **Bias** — generative models reflect and can amplify biases in their training data (e.g., stereotyped depictions). The fairness lessons from Foundations apply directly.
- **Consent** — using someone's likeness or an artist's style without permission is an ethical (and increasingly legal) problem.
- **Provenance** — knowing whether content is AI-made. Approaches include **watermarking** generated outputs and content-credential standards (like C2PA) that attach a verifiable "made by AI" label.

<Callout type="warning">
As a builder, you're responsible for what your systems can do. Add guardrails against generating harmful content, be transparent that content is AI-generated, respect consent and copyright, and watermark outputs where you can. "I just built the tool" is not a sufficient answer when the tool causes harm.
</Callout>
