A **Generative Adversarial Network (GAN)** generates startlingly realistic data through competition. Two networks play a game: one tries to fake data, the other tries to catch the fakes. As each improves, it forces the other to improve — until the fakes are indistinguishable from real.

## The two players

- **Generator** — the forger. Takes random noise and tries to produce realistic data (e.g., a fake face). It never sees real data directly; it only learns from the detective's feedback.
- **Discriminator** — the detective. Sees both real data and the generator's fakes, and learns to tell them apart, outputting a probability "real."

They train together in a tug-of-war: the discriminator gets better at spotting fakes, which pushes the generator to make better fakes, which pushes the discriminator again. This is **adversarial** training.

The discriminator's verdict is just a thresholded probability.

## The generator's goal: fool the detective

The generator succeeds when the discriminator labels its fakes as "real." We can measure progress by counting how many fakes slipped past.

<CodeExercise
  id="gan-fooled"
  prompt="The discriminator scored four generated fakes. Count how many fooled it (scored above 0.5 = 'real')."
  starter={`scores = [0.6, 0.3, 0.8, 0.2]
# Count how many fakes were classified as real (score > 0.5)
`}
  expected="2"
  solution={`scores = [0.6, 0.3, 0.8, 0.2]
print(sum(1 for s in scores if s > 0.5))`}
/>

Two of four fooled the detective — the generator is learning. At convergence, the discriminator can do no better than a coin flip (0.5 everywhere): the fakes have become indistinguishable from real.

## The catch: GANs are hard to train

That elegant game is also temperamental:

- **Instability** — the two networks can oscillate instead of converging; if one overpowers the other, learning stalls.
- **Mode collapse** — the generator finds one or two outputs that reliably fool the discriminator and just produces those, losing variety (e.g., only ever generating one face).

GANs produce sharp, impressive images and were state of the art for years. But their training headaches opened the door to a more stable approach that now dominates image generation — diffusion.

<Callout type="tip">
The GAN idea — two networks improving by opposition — is one of the most influential in deep learning, beyond images. But for text-to-image generation today, diffusion models have largely taken over because they're more stable and controllable.
</Callout>
