**Diffusion models** power the image generators reshaping art and design. The core idea is beautifully counterintuitive: if a model can learn to *remove* a little noise from an image, you can run it over and over to turn **pure random noise into a brand-new image**.

## Two directions

- **Forward process (destroy):** start with a real image and add a little random **noise**, step by step, until it's pure static. This part needs no learning — it's just adding noise.
- **Reverse process (create):** train a model to **undo** one step of noising — to predict and remove the noise. Run that learned denoiser repeatedly, starting from random static, and a coherent image gradually emerges.

The model's entire job is: *given a noisy image, predict the noise that was added.* Subtract it, and you've taken one step back toward a clean image.

Let's see the forward step — adding noise to an image (here, a 2-pixel "image").

And the reverse step — if the model correctly predicts the noise, subtracting it recovers the clean image.

<CodeExercise
  id="diff-denoise"
  prompt="Reverse step: subtract the predicted noise from the noisy image to recover the original."
  starter={`noisy = [11, 18]
predicted_noise = [1, -2]
# Print the denoised image (noisy minus predicted noise)
`}
  expected="[10, 20]"
  solution={`noisy = [11, 18]
predicted_noise = [1, -2]
print([a - b for a, b in zip(noisy, predicted_noise)])`}
/>

We recovered `[10, 20]`. Real generation does this over many small steps, each removing a bit of noise, until a detailed image appears from the static.

## Why diffusion won

Compared to GANs, diffusion models are:

- **More stable to train** — no adversarial tug-of-war; it's a straightforward "predict the noise" objective.
- **More diverse** — no mode collapse; they cover the full variety of the data.
- **Higher quality** — the step-by-step refinement produces fine detail.

The trade-off is **speed**: many denoising steps make generation slower than a GAN's single pass. **Latent diffusion** (used by Stable Diffusion) fixes most of this by running the whole process in a compact **latent space** (from the autoencoder lesson!) instead of on full-size pixels.

<Callout type="tip">
The denoiser network is usually a <strong>U-Net</strong> — the same shrink-then-expand architecture used for image segmentation in the Computer Vision track. Diffusion stitches together ideas from across this whole curriculum: latent spaces, U-Nets, and (next) embeddings for text guidance.
</Callout>
