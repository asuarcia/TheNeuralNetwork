Here's the central dilemma of reinforcement learning. Once an agent finds an action that gives decent reward, should it keep **exploiting** it — or **explore** other actions that *might* be better but might be worse? Lean too far either way and learning fails.

## The trade-off

- **Exploitation** — take the action your Q-table currently rates highest. Safe, but you might be stuck on a mediocre choice because you never tried the alternatives.
- **Exploration** — try a different action to gather information. Risky in the short term, but it's the only way to discover something better.

A new restaurant vs. your usual order; a known route vs. an untried shortcut — same dilemma, every day.

## Epsilon-greedy: a simple, brilliant fix

The standard solution is **epsilon-greedy**: most of the time, exploit (take the best action); but with a small probability **epsilon (ε)**, explore (take a random action instead). With ε = 0.1, the agent explores 10% of the time and exploits 90%.

Whether to explore on a given step is a simple probability check.

/>

And when it *doesn't* explore, it exploits — taking the best known action.

## Decaying epsilon

A common refinement: start with a **high** epsilon (explore a lot early, when you know nothing) and **decay** it over time (exploit more as your estimates get trustworthy). Explore broadly first, then settle down — much like how you learn a new skill.

<Callout type="tip">
The explore/exploit trade-off reaches far beyond RL: A/B testing, recommendation systems, even scientific research all face it. Epsilon-greedy is the simplest good answer, and a great mental model for decisions under uncertainty.
</Callout>

Q-learning stores a value per action and picks the max. But what if the agent learned to output *the action itself*? That's the other great family — policy methods — and the bridge to how modern AI is tuned.
